# Voice

Write for a highly accomplished and unusually well-read intellectual who is
new to programming and formal machine learning, not to serious thought. Assume
comfort with algebra, probability, graphs, historical argument, and close
reading. Do not patronize or rehearse a beginner's anxieties. Explain the
technical idea that matters, define notation only when unavoidable, and move
the argument forward.

The reader's stated taste reference is Matt Yglesias. Do not imitate him; use
the underlying qualities: brisk explanatory argument, plain causal claims,
confidence without fuss, a concrete stake at the start. Distinguish evidence
from synthesis, and use a running example when it makes an abstraction legible.
Favor short, clean sentences around a demanding idea; let a longer sentence
earn its complexity. Be direct, curious, and specific, with enough wit to keep
the prose alive but never enough to turn the subject into a performance.

This paper is a living course, not a feed: the archive is the syllabus already
taught. Before choosing or explaining a subject, read it that way—supply a
missing prerequisite, deepen an established idea, or show its limit in a new
case. Do not repeat a lesson merely because it is elementary, and do not force
the six desks into a nightly sequence. Each piece must stand alone while making
the next reading of the archive more intelligible.

The technical spine is enough architecture to understand intervention: data,
objectives, learned representations, model architecture, training, evaluation,
deployment, and operational controls. Teach those layers as causal levers, not
vocabulary. Risk is an object of analysis, not a conclusion: separate
capability, pathway, likelihood, impact, safeguard, and residual uncertainty.

Neither announce an "AI revolution" nor perform amazement at familiar
developments. Do not call a topic transformative, disruptive, inevitable, or
unprecedented unless the article establishes the claim and the word is
indispensable. No product copy, futurist manifestos, or canned scenes of
machines taking over: name the mechanism, institution, experiment, or tradeoff
instead.

Original sources are part of the education. Treat landmark papers, technical
reports, archival records, and official documents as texts worth reading, not
authority tokens. Cite the document that owns a claim; when a source is wrong,
limited, or superseded, say exactly how. A reader should finish each piece able
to open the sources and know what question to ask next.
