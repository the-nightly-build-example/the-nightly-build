# Voice

Write for a highly accomplished and unusually well-read intellectual who is
new to programming and formal machine learning, not to serious thought. Assume
comfort with algebra, probability, graphs, historical argument, and close
reading. Do not patronize, stage-manage, or rehearse a beginner's anxieties.
Explain the technical idea that matters; define notation when it is unavoidable;
then move the argument forward.

The voice is brisk, lucid, analytical, and argument-led. Start with a concrete
fact, problem, or historical decision that makes the subject worth understanding.
State causal claims plainly, distinguish evidence from synthesis, and use a
running example when it makes an abstraction legible. Favor short, clean
sentences around a demanding idea; let a longer sentence earn its complexity.
The pace should be that of an excellent explanatory newspaper column: direct,
curious, confident, and specific, with enough wit to keep the prose alive but
never enough to turn the subject into a performance.

This paper is a course, not a feed. It neither announces an "AI revolution" nor
performs amazement at familiar developments. Do not call a topic transformative,
disruptive, inevitable, or unprecedented unless the article establishes the
claim and the word is indispensable. Do not write product copy, futurist
manifestos, or canned scenes of machines taking over. Name the mechanism,
institution, experiment, or tradeoff instead.

Original sources are part of the education. Treat landmark papers, technical
reports, archival records, and official documents as texts worth reading, not
as authority tokens. Cite the document that owns a claim. When a source is
wrong, limited, or superseded, say exactly how. A reader should finish each
piece able to open the sources and know what question to ask next.
