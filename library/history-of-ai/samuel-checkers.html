Arthur Samuel's checkers program learned by chasing its own deeper guess

On an IBM 701 with a few thousand words of storage, Samuel's program already separated memorizing positions from generalizing over them, and it never beat the champion the legend later handed it.

9 min read2026-07-17

A program that learned to beat its author

In 1959 Arthur Samuel published a paper in the IBM Journal of Research and Development with a plain, startling claim in its abstract: a computer could be programmed "so that it will learn to play a better game of checkers than can be played by the person who wrote the program," and could learn to do it "in a remarkably short period of time (8 or 10 hours of machine-playing time)."1 The phrase in the title was "machine learning." Under it sat something that had until then been mostly a promise: a program that got better from its own experience, running on real hardware, with the results written down.

The lineage was already several years old. Samuel wrote the first operating checkers program for the IBM 701 in 1952, recoded it for the IBM 704 in 1954, finished the first version that learned in 1955, and demonstrated it on television on February 24, 1956.1 By one much-repeated account, Thomas J. Watson Sr. predicted the television demonstration would raise IBM's stock fifteen points, and it did.2 Samuel was not the first to put checkers on a computer; Christopher Strachey had run a checkers program on the Ferranti Mark I in 1951. His claim was narrower and more durable. It was about learning.2

The paper described two ways the program improved: rote learning, which memorized positions it had seen, and learning by generalization, which adjusted a scoring rule so it applied to positions it had not. The distinction between memorizing and generalizing is one its modern descendants still teach, and Samuel drew it on a machine holding a few thousand words of memory. The famous story that the program later beat a champion outran what the record shows.

Packing the board into four words

The design was shaped by what the machine physically was. IBM's own account of the 701 describes its memory as "electrostatic devices called Williams tubes," each holding a charge pattern equivalent to about 20,000 digits, backed by magnetic drums and tape.3 In the units an engineer of the day would quote, that came to 2,048 words of 36 bits each, expandable to 4,096, with a memory cycle of 12 microseconds.4 A few kilobytes held the whole working state.

A checkers position had to fit inside that. Samuel packed one board into four 36-bit machine words, mapping the playable squares onto individual bit positions.1 Scoring a position with the fuller evaluation rule took about 7.5 milliseconds; making a move and finding the resulting board took another millisecond and a half.1 Self-play meant scoring thousands of positions per game at those rates, which is why learning ran in hours rather than seconds, and why every later choice about how much to remember and how far to look was really a choice about milliseconds and machine words.

What memorizing could not reach

The simpler method was to remember. As the program played, it saved each board position it evaluated together with the value it had backed up for that position from a look-ahead search, and it stored the pair on a memory tape. When it met a saved position again, it could substitute the value it had already worked out, in effect recalling the result of a search instead of repeating it.1 The tape came to hold "something over 53,000 board positions," which sharpened the program's opening and endgame play where the same situations recurred.1

The ceiling was arithmetic. Memorizing scales with the number of distinct positions, and checkers has far more than a tape can hold. Samuel estimated that master-caliber play by rote alone would require more than a million words of storage, and judged the game "not completely amenable to this treatment on the IBM 704."1 Rote learning was real learning of a narrow kind. It made the program faster and steadier on ground it had already covered. It could do nothing at all with a position it had never stored, and most positions are ones it had never stored. That gap is what the second method was built to close.

The scoring rule that corrected itself

To act on positions it had never seen, the program needed a rule rather than a table. Samuel scored a board with an evaluation polynomial: a weighted sum of hand-chosen features such as piece advantage, mobility, and control of the center. The rote-era rule used four such terms; the generalization version used 16 active terms selected from a pool of 38 parameters, with piece advantage computed separately.1

Evaluation polynomial A weighted sum of board features that the program maximizes as a stand-in, a proxy, for the thing it cannot compute directly: whether a position ultimately wins.

The learning was a way of tuning that proxy against itself. To choose a move, the program looked ahead about three moves, extending some forced lines much deeper, and backed up the best reachable score to the current position under the assumption of good play by both sides.12 Then came the adjustment. At each of its own moves the program compared the score it had recorded for the board one move earlier against the backed-up score the deeper look-ahead now assigned the current position. It called that difference delta. When delta was positive, the terms that had voted for the earlier position were given more weight and the terms that had voted against it were given less; when it was negative, the reverse.1 The program was trying, in Samuel's words, "to make the score, calculated for the current board position, look like that calculated for the terminal board position of the chain of moves which most probably will occur during actual play."

It trained by playing itself. One copy, Alpha, adjusted its polynomial after every move; the other, Beta, held a fixed polynomial for the length of a game and inherited Alpha's whenever Alpha won.1 Over eight to ten hours the weights settled toward a shallow estimate that agreed with the program's own deeper look. Moving a current estimate toward a later, deeper estimate of the same quantity is what Richard Sutton and Andrew Barto, decades afterward, would identify as an early use of temporal-difference learning.5 That reading is theirs, from a theory Samuel did not have. What he had in 1959 was the mechanism, and the reason for it was thrift: he could not afford to search deep on every move, so he made a cheap estimate carry the weight of an expensive one and taught it from the difference.

Whom the program actually beat

Samuel was candid about how well it played. He graded the learning program as "a rather better-than-average novice, but definitely not as an expert," and reported that amateurs who faced it called it "tricky but beatable."1 The record after that is where the popular telling and the primary documents part ways.

In the summer of 1962, running on an IBM 7094 rather than the 1950s 701 or 704, the program won a single game against Robert Nealey, a blind checkers master from Connecticut.24 IBM's contemporary Research News billed Nealey as "a former Connecticut checkers champion, and one of the nation's foremost players." As Jonathan Schaeffer documents, that billing was inflated: Nealey had not won the Connecticut title at the time of the game, he took it only in 1966, four years later; his tournament results "never justified" the master claim; and he had never played the U.S. championship.6 Martin Fierz reaches the same verdict, that Nealey "was not half as good as was claimed."7 IBM's own present-day history page has quietly retreated to calling him a "self-described 'checkers master.'"8

Samuel's 1967 sequel supplies the ceiling in his own hand. Its abstract reports the program "still unable to outplay checker masters."9 A six-game rematch against Nealey ended with the machine achieving five draws and one loss, so it did not beat him again. In 1965 the World Champion, W. F. Hellman, won all four games played by mail, though he was played to a draw in one hurriedly played crossboard game, and Pacific Coast Champion K. D. Hanson beat later versions on two occasions.9 At a 1966 exhibition tied to the world championship the program lost all eight of its crossboard games to Hellman and Derek Oldbury.672 The legend hands the program a champion. The record gives it one win over a strong blind amateur whose reputation had been oversold, and losses to every genuine master it faced.

The ancestor already understood

What Samuel built on a few thousand words of memory was not a champion. It was the first working case of a program that improved from its own play, and the distinctions it had to draw to do that are the ones the subject still organizes around. Memorizing against generalizing: rote learning was quick but bounded by storage, and generalization was the part that could handle a position never seen before. A proxy objective: the polynomial stood in for winning, which the program could not compute, and the work was keeping that stand-in honest against a deeper search. The delta update, moving a shallow estimate toward a deeper one, was the ancestor of a method taught today under another name.

The constraints were the design. Kilobytes of memory forced the choice between remembering positions and generalizing over them. Milliseconds per position forced a shallow search. A shallow search forced the delta update that let the estimate teach itself, because deep look-ahead was not affordable. Judge it as 1959 could: a better-than-average player, no more, and Samuel said so first.1 The value is not in the games it won. It is that the problems its modern catalogue teaches, generalization against memorization and an objective that is only a proxy for the goal, were already built and already understood as problems, on a machine that could barely hold the board.
